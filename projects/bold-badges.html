Research Details

Topic Research

We researched the motivations and habits of people over the age of 65. We attended and participated in workout classes for seniors, where we interviewed them after the classes – asking them qualitative and quantitative questions.

What motivates seniors to work out?

Seniors are in two main groups:
1. They have a long history of working out and continue to do so because it makes them feel good
2. Their only form of working-out are brisk walks

What kind of fitness markers are they tracking?

- Seniors are not aware of modern fitness tracking. 
- The only fitness markers they do track are their weight and blood pressure

Product Research

We conducted research into similar services, analyzing UI, UX, User flow, key features and reviews to identify the common problems.
We did competitive research into: Silver Sneakers.  And, comparative research into: Peloton, FitBit, Apple Watch, Under Armor, and Khan Academy

Research Insights

Our user interviews revealed:
- Our target audience needed to be narrowed
- Users are only likely to use an online fitness program if it was easy to understand
- Their fitness history heavily impacted their comfort level with online fitness programs
- Their career impacted their comfort levels with using online platform

Key Quotes Directly from Users:

- “I only track how far I ride on my bike, nothing else”
- “I only walk to exercise”
- “I’m not very sedentary, I don’t like to sit for too long.  I don’t like being stiff”
- “I am in tune with my body and it’s needs, I don’t need to track anything”

Design Principles

After our research and user interviews, we created a list of design principles for this project.  We sought to ensure that our research/interview insights would inform our design decisions, and to directly influence our end product.

Approachable

Most seniors have never used an online fitness program, some said they were uncomfortable with the concept. Therefor, the experience has to be approachable, friendly and welcoming.

Simple

A good portion of users aren’t familiar with online dashboards, most only having used Facebook or Yahoo.  Having a simple and intuitive interface is paramount with this user group.

Personal

Many seniors mentioned appreciating the “personal touch” that working with trainers provided, having a personalized dashboard with personalized messages creates a sense of familiarity and relatability with the service.

Design Principles

After our research and user interviews, we created a list of design principles for this project. We sought to ensure that our research/interview insights would inform our design decisions, and to directly influence our end product.

Most seniors have never used an online fitness program, some said they were uncomfortable with the concept.  Therefor, the experience has to be approachable, friendly and welcoming.

A good portion of users aren’t familiar with online dashboards, most only having used Facebook or Yahoo.  Having a simple and intuitive interface is paramount with this user group.

Personal

Many seniors mentioned appreciating the “personal touch” that working with trainers provided, having a personalized dashboard with personalized messages creates a sense of familiarity and relatability with the service. 

Ideation & Dot Voting

With our Design Principles in place, we spent a day and did Crazy 8 sketches for ideation.
- We came up with 40 low-fidelity mockups for a fitness dashboard
- We reached out to our peers and voted on the best features of each design
- We met with the client for feedback on the viability of each design

Design Patterns We Prioritized:

Based on the feedback from our peers & the client, we proposed a minimal, swiss-grid to organize and contain the dashboard’s information. Synthesizing the research & feedback, we reached a consensus for what information was pertinent to the users & the business.
Next Up Video: A large video player that quickly captures their attention.  This allows users to seamlessly continue their program with little friction.  
Member View Counter: Social proof motivates other users to follow suit and join the fun!
Personalized Welcome Message: Welcomes the user to their personalized workout routine
Program Tracker: Visualization of workout program process, helps visually reinforce the end goal of finishing the programBadge
Gallery: Visualization of the user’s recent successes; shows them the recent badges they have collected – this positive reinforcement promotes users to continue with the program
Bold Tips: Seniors wanted to see more information regarding fitness, so we listened and added this function, they love it!

First Prototype

Concluding Validation Testing

With our first prototype, we went back into the field to perform more testing.  To increase the diversity of our testing base, we performed tests at the Ferry Building instead of a senior-fitness class.  Based on this usability testing, all users found the dashboard very intuitive and easy to navigate.

Second Prototype

Here’s what we addressed:

Synthesizing the testing results, we went back to work and made some changes to our prototype:
– We changed some copy to use terms they are more familiar with
– We altered some visuals to increase legibility
– We removed smaller visual elements they assumed they should be able to read, but didn’t need to

Some things we could do better:

Select usability participants at more locations
– Usability testing with a wider variety of copy
– Pushed to test prototype with current Bold users

Design Case Study

Designing digital achievements for seniors
I recently had the pleasure of working on an online senior-fitness service with Age Bold. I had the opportunity to design a brand new gamification system with a team of designers.
2019 / Age Bold / Online Fitness Badge System

Problem & Goal

Bold needed a badge system; we designed and illustrated one from the ground up.

As the company scales up, users need the ability to interact with even more information. Helping seniors presented us with a different set of challenges (compared to the typical fitness platform user). We aimed to elucidate their unique needs, and to design a dashboard that accessibly displayed relevant information.

The Challenge

Designing their first dashboard would present these challenges:
• Seniors unfamiliar with complex UI’s
• Users not comprehending how to use an online fitness tracker
• Different set of needs (from seniors) to anticipate and understand
• Creating a complex, but intuitive homepage

The Goal

• Build a welcoming UI to increase user acquisition
• Research what categories of information seniors wanted to see (regarding their fitness)
• Prevent situations where users may ask, “How do I use this platform?”
• Test for and remove any pain-points to help Bold increase user loyalty

Research Results

Users already knew what they wanted to find, but they felt that most dashboards were too difficult to comprehend

Our research revealed that seniors had a very good understanding of what they were looking for in an online fitness platform. But, none of them had ever used one, many users didn't even know such platforms existed. Furthermore, every user felt that online-dashboards were unintuitive. For these two reasons, they either weren't aware of fitness-platforms, and even if they did, felt they were inaccessible [to them].

Retrospective

When designing something new, reaching out to your network of peers is imperative...

I now understand how difficult it is to introduce a crucial feature to a company’s product within a niche market space; we needed to consider new lines of questioning with the users and with our own teammates. Working closely with the Bold team was a very fulfilling experience, I have enhanced my understanding in a broad spectrum of topics such as design thinking and interpersonal design-skills.

Useful resources during project:

1.  Reach out to your network of peers
2. Frequently revisiting these books: UX Strategy, The User Experience Team of One, and The Elements of User Experience
3. Use a Design Journal, along with a Bullet Journal

Utilizing these resources:

• Create Keynotes to summarize current project status (to present during team meetings)
• Set up Crazy 8 sketches as a team
• Use plenty of sticky notes in your books and design-journals
