Age Bold Personalized Dashboard

project Overiview

Designing a fitness dashboard for seniors

"Waking Up" is a mobile meditation app. Users have access to a variety of audio tracks, they vary in topics, styles, and difficulties. There currently isn't a clear way to differentiate between these categories. Furthermore, there isn't an easy way to find where you previously left off. This has left users frustrated and lost while exploring the variety of audio tracks.

Finally, when users stick around and find their path to meditative success, there isn't an in-app function to share one's progress.

Research

RESEARCH AND DISCOVERY

Leveraging usability testing to uncover areas of friction when exploring lessons.

I tested 6 users in San Francisco with the initial Waking Up app. It was important for me to focus on research since this application is geared towards experience and inexperience meditators – I had to understand very different potential users. I conducted user interviews, created two personas, research our competitors and other companies in the meditation space. And, used the "Jobs to be Done" framework as part of my research process.

I split the users into two categories, experience and inexperienced. The inexperienced were designated with sifting through the library of lessons, while the experienced were given the task of sharing their progress.

INSIGHTS FROM USABILITY TESTING

People are interested in meditating, but many find the apps uninteresting and unintuitive.

Synthesizing pain points

Making retention of new users as seamless as possible

I set out to accomplish 3 objectives that were informed by information I gathered from usability testing. By arranging multiple pain points in a 2x2 plot, I was able to come to these three primary areas of focus:

  • Have new users be able to easily understand what lessons are available to them
  • Get users to quickly organize and sift through lessons they enjoyed
  • Increase user retention with positive incentives (awards and progress)

Pain Point 1: Uninspiring Onboarding

The onboarding process is important for users to understand what lessons are available to them. Currently there is no implementation besides a "Start Here" video (which only briefly explains how meditation works).

Furthermore, all users, regardless if they are paying, experienced, or a beginner, are funneled into the exact same homepage. This home page stays the same regardless how long the user has used the app.

Pain Point 2: Lack of Search and Filter

Meditation apps are generally only audio based, Waking Up is the same – it is a large library of different audio tracks. I noticed that users were having a difficult time sifting through the library of tracks. They had difficulty understanding which track was appropiate to them. Furthermore, they had difficulty finding audio tracks based on their current context.

In some scenario's, a user was only provided a small amount of time to listen to a potential track, they had a difficult time finding a short track for the alotted time.

In other scenario's, a user was given the task of finding a track they liked, they had a very difficult time finding said track again. 

Given these issues, it was necessary to introduce a function that would allow them to sort through the tracks with finer details.

Feedback From Testing

Users Like Having Control When it's Easy to See
Users I tested were surprised the "filter" function was not in the current version of the app. They were even more surprised that a "search" function was not present.

Put the Important Things at the Top
Everyone I tested would immediately check the top of the page. If a function was important, it was better to make it visible and place it near the top of the page – they would use that more frequently.

Pain Point 3: Lacks Sharing or Progress

Users fell into two categories, people that do not meditate but want to, and people that already meditate. Most new users are excited at learning about meditation, but find some apps uninspiring and lack the necessary tools to help motivate them.

By implementing functions to track and share your progress. Waking Up would retain users through positive incentives.

I made a few iterations of sharing and tracking one's progress. I decided to use all the interations and combined what was strongest in each of them.

Feedback From Testing

We Like Shiny Things
We react well to positive encouragement, we react even better when that information is displayed visually. Currently, Waking Up only displays the number of minutes a user has meditatated. Many users I tested even glanced over this information. Introducing a visual graph to track one's progress would reinforce one's meditative practice, but would also serve as an image to share with others.

Final Design 🏆

The final design focused in on three main things: introducing a search and filter function, a progress bar, a visual awards system, and finally, the ability to share said progress with others.

Feedback From Testing

Convetion Works
After numerous iterations, it is clear that convention isn't necessarily bad – it actually works really well for a reason. People are accustomed to how other application work, so to play of this existing knowledge and to introduce things they have seen elsewhere was effective for improving the user experience.

Final thoughts

Importance of User Research

By testing a variety of users in the field, I was able to discover additional pain points I wouldn't have otherwise.  Frequent testing in the field was invaluable to the discovery phase of this project.

Every app has issues, even the "big" ones

Waking Up, by Sam Harris, is created by MetaLabs, the same company that works with Coinbase.  In spite of the scale of MetaLabs, there were still basic functions that Waking Up was missing. This is why it is always important to research and test your apps, even if it's backed by a big name.
